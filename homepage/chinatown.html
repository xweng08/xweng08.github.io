My Write Your New York Essay

Sunset

November 4, 2024

Looking back, writing this essay was pretty fun. Definitely not my best work though! The 1000 word limit on this essay really screwed me over.

I can never remember the first time I ended up in this neighborhood. Since I came to New York, I’ve been constantly moving from place to place. One day, I could be in the tall apartments of Queens. On the other, I’d find my parents packing everything we had into suitcases and telling us we were going on an adventure–this was always the case no matter where we lived. After a couple of years, when the landlord raised the rent too high, I’d be forced to pack my whole life into a single suitcase and move somewhere else. There was no telling how far away the place would be, so I never really bothered getting attached to anywhere I lived. Half because it’d be a hassle to do so and half because I felt detached from the city. No matter where I ended up, there seemed to be nothing to connect to. To the eyes of my preschool classmates, I was the odd one out, the weird little girl who couldn’t speak English, the girl who had pale skin too, but not of the same hue. One of those years was the year I moved to Sunset Park.

Sunset Park has always historically housed immigrants, with over half of its residents being foreign-born. This history is reflected in the feel of the neighborhood; it doesn’t give off the touristy Manhattan New York vibe but instead radiates traces of an old country left behind by its residents in hopes of better prospects. The day I moved into Sunset Park was the day it showed me its beauty. As I helped my parents lug the suitcases out of the dingy, narrow Forty-Fifth Street station on Fourth Avenue and make our way up the hill that was Fifth and Sixth Avenue, from its stores to the residents, I was amazed by the variety of Sunset Park. The Fourth and Fifth Avenues, overflowing with delis, bodegas, family-owned restaurants, and storefronts, contrasted with the Seventh through Eighth Avenues, filled with medical services, grocery stores, bakeries, and restaurants lined up beside each other.

For me, this was the charm of Sunset Park–the quality that made it feel like home. To me, my Sunset Park is the Sunset of Eighth Avenue. The predominantly Asian demographic of the avenue fostered a sense of familiarity in me. Maybe the only reason I place such importance on this specific part of Sunset is because it bears striking resemblances to my hometown. Through the noisiness of Eighth Avenue, I saw glimpses of the motherland, and it looked back at me and enveloped me with a sense of comfort and belonging. The first time I stepped into the avenue, I was struck by how much chaos can be packed into a strip of land. My home is in the avenue of overly aggressive clothing store clerks, vendors shouting about sales on their fish or vegetables to attract attention, grannies trying to bargain for cheaper deals, the perpetual puddles of unknown liquids at the side of the road from workers pouring dirty water into the sewage drains, products and produce practically taking up half of the sidewalk, and the putrid smell of fish I’ve never been able to get over.

Of course, the neighborhood isn’t just Eighth Avenue. As much as Sunset Park is a home to me, it’s also a home to all its residents. You look around in this working-class neighborhood brimming with immigrants under the poverty line, and you can see what this neighborhood means to them. Sunset Park housed them and took them in in this crowded, dizzying city. In this neighborhood, they found familiarity and belonging in its diversity and the comfort of others who understood them and shared their culture. Sunset runs on the strive of its residents and their hope to build a better life for themselves and their children. Sunset Park not only houses an Asian community but also a large population of Hispanics and Latinos. This big neighborhood is split in two, with the local park at the heart of it all. Each half is distinct in its atmosphere and the people. We have different cuisines, holidays, languages, and traditions. But as different as the two dominant halves of the population in Sunset are, it’s a place we gladly call home at the end of the day. We are brought here and together by a common goal and ambition and are part of the cogs that make New York City, New York City.

Although I can never seem to remember the first time I ended up in this neighborhood, the year I moved into Sunset Park, I became captivated by it. That year, I was no longer the oddball in class. It was the year I found a place I belonged in the city. And suddenly, whenever the time came to move, I would beg my parents to find a place in the neighborhood.
